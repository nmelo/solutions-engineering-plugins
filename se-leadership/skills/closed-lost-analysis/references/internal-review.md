# The internal learning conversation

The conversation this skill produces an agenda for is the selling team's internal review of the evaluation outcome — the same internal review the process specifies after the customer-facing readout meeting (Phase 5.2) has already happened. Once the deal is a Technical Loss, that internal review takes on a learning purpose. The manager joins as facilitator.

The customer is not in this conversation. The Project Sponsor and Executive Sponsor are not in this conversation. They were in Phase 5.2 — their input lives in the readout and in the recordings of that meeting.

## Who's in the room

| Role | Why they're here |
|---|---|
| The SE who ran the evaluation | Co-created the readout. Carries the technical narrative the readout is recording. |
| The AE who owned the account | Co-created the readout. Carries the commercial narrative and the relationship history. |
| The manager, as facilitator | Keeps the conversation anchored to the readout. Brings no pre-formed theory. |

Keep the room small. Pulling in adjacent functions (product, marketing, leadership) at this stage turns a learning conversation into a stakeholder briefing. Those audiences can receive the captured learning afterward; they should not be present while it is being derived.

## What the manager brings in

- **The readout document, in front of everyone.** The conversation reads it together. It is not summarized from memory.
- **The original Execution Plan.** The readout's section-by-section outcomes are being measured against the original plan. Without the plan, the comparison can't run.
- **The recordings or notes from the customer-facing readout meeting (Phase 5.2).** This is the customer's own words on why they decided as they did. The team's interpretation of the loss should be checked against what the customer actually said.
- **No pre-formed theory of why the deal was lost.** The conversation derives the learning. It does not import it. A manager who walks in with "I think we lost because X" produces a conversation that confirms X, not one that surfaces what the readout actually shows.

## Sequencing — readout-anchored

Walk the readout section by section, following the seven-section mapping in the main SKILL.md. Each section produces one learning question. The team answers it from evidence in the readout and the recordings — not from memory, not from impression, not from what felt off at the time.

This is the discipline. A conversation that wanders off the readout becomes a blame conversation by default. A conversation that stays on the readout becomes an evidence conversation by default. The manager's main move is to keep the conversation anchored to what the readout says, not to where the SE or AE *felt* things went wrong.

When the conversation strays — "I think the real issue was…" — the manager's redirect is the same shape every time: *"Where does the readout show that? Let's look at the section."* If the readout doesn't show it, the team has surfaced a gap in what they recorded, which is itself the learning. If the readout does show it, the conversation continues on evidence.

## What the manager does not do

- Does not bring a root-cause framework into the conversation. The seven section-mapped questions are the structure. Anything more is out of scope for this skill — see Known Limitations in the SKILL.md.
- Does not assign blame to individuals. The detectors point to *planning-stage* failures the loss made visible — those are team and process findings, not personal ones.
- Does not adjudicate product performance in the room. If the readout shows a product issue, the learning is to route it to product, not to litigate it among the selling team.
- Does not promise where the captured learning goes next. Routing learnings back to sales, product, or SE practice is out of scope for this skill — that mechanism is a v2 signal, not a v0.1 capability.

## After the conversation

The output of the internal review is a small set of findings, each grounded in a specific readout section, each tied to a specific captured lesson. The findings live with the team that produced them. Where they go after that — back to sales practice, back to product, back to SE training, into a cross-loss pattern review — is a separate workflow the skill does not cover.

If the team finishes the conversation and wants to feed the findings somewhere, the manager notes the destination and the team agrees the handoff offline. The internal review is the input to that next step, not the step itself.
